(If you're unfamiliar with today's topic, then perhaps best stick this one out... Otherwise, here is as good a place to start as any.)

As an unlikely political candidate in a country far from my own, Paul holds no direct relevance for me. However, he has come to personify something much larger than himself and is representative of a growing libertarian movement -- largely in the US, but also present in other places around the world -- which may or may not peak in numbers before it reaches the necessary critical mass to truly affect public policy. I don't wish to debate the merits of libertarianism here; not least the particular brand that Paul espouses. For what it's worth, I am certainly very sympathetic to a number of his arguments, such as those on foreign policy and the asinine war on drugs. I even consider myself less authoritarian than him on the subject of immigration. (He proposes quite strict border controls, which runs counter to most notions of libertarianism that I am familiar with). On economics and some other issues, it's true, we would find a lot more to disagree on. I have no desire to return to a gold standard or repeal the practice of fractional reserve banking, while it appears that the gilded scales will never fall from Dr Paul's eyes.

All that being said, I am interested in the ideas of freedom and prosperity, even to the extent that people might disagree over the fundamental meanings of those concepts. (See Will Wilkinson's excellent post on this matter.) And, just as Paul is a now a symbol of some bigger movement, the bigotted ramblings in two-decades old newsletters hold greater significance

Personally, what is in the newsletters has become something of a side issue. The response to them is what really gets to me.

Crouching tigers, circled wagons
The surprisingly elusive question of who actually wrote the offensive pieces appears somewhat closer to being resolved --  at least to my mind. My growing suspicion over passed few months (recently fanned by Bob Murphy and then all-but-comfirmed by Gene Callaghan) is that the odious remarks were written, not by Lew Rockwell, but Murray Rothbard.[*]

I initially thought that everyone in the Paul camp was being evasive -- or, worse, defensive -- because acknowledging those newsletters would be tantamount to admitting all those nasty things that the critics of libertarianism always claimed it was. That is, a self-serving ideology promoted by rich white bigots under false pretences that everyone stood a fair chance to do as well as them.

And, I'm sorry, but saying you don't know who wrote them and disavow them simply isn't good enough.

But herein lies the rub; no-one in the Paul party has been willing to cede some kind of responsibility about the matter.
