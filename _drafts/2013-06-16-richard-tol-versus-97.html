(2013), which has received widespread attention for "quantifying the consensus on anthropogenic global warming (AGW)". In short, Cook and his colleagues find overwhelming support (+/- 97%) for the theory of man-made climate change within the scientific literature.

Of course, the qualitative results should hardly come as a surprise to anyone who isn't living in an alternate reality populated by the likes of Fox News and Alex Jones. Nevertheless, the paper has elicited a noisy response from the sceptic community. I'll get to that in a second, but first some important background on the Cook study, which has two distinct phases:

Most obviously, the author self-ratings show a much lower proclivity towards a "No position" stance (first column)...

As I said, there has been a vigorous response to the Cook paper from the contrarian corners of the internet. For instance, a huge fuss was made about several papers that were ostensibly rated incorrectly. That a cherry-picked subsample  of N<5 was being used to question the representivity of a sample totaling over ten thousand observations is almost beyond parody... But I suspect that statistical-based irony is beyond those making the claim.

However, a more interesting critique has come in the form of the economist, Richard Tol. I should say upfront that Tol is not a climate sceptic and has a long history arguing for carbon taxes. (Although, I do find him to be weirdly sycophantic to the denial faction at times.)

There has been a looong discussion taking place in the comments section of the Wottsupwiththat blog, which I have been involved in. I'll summarise the main takeaways from this conversation in my next blog.
